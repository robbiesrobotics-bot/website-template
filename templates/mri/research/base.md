# Insurance Restoration Industry Intelligence Brief
## Modern Remodeling / Insurance Restoration — Northern Virginia Market

---

## 1. Common Services Offered

### Roof Replacement & Repair
- **Asphalt shingles**: most common residential roofing material; 20–30 year lifespan; $8K–$18K for full replacement on a 2,000 sq ft roof in NoVA
- **Metal roofing**: rising demand for longevity and storm resistance; $15K–$40K+ installed; often favored in hail-prone areas
- **Slate roofing**: premium tier; $25K–$60K+ installed; not all contractors install it; signals high-end capability
- **Flat / TPO / EPDM**: commercial and some residential; different set of certifications and techniques
- Insurance-triggered roof replacements are the single largest revenue driver for restoration contractors; hail and wind events are the primary catalysts

### Siding Replacement
- **Vinyl siding**: most popular; $12K–$30K for full wrap on a 2,500 sq ft home; low maintenance; wide color/style range
- **Fiber cement (James Hardie, CertainTeed)**: premium; $18K–$45K installed; wood-like aesthetic; flame and rot resistant; the brand "James Hardie" is a known consumer item that homeowners request by name
- **Aluminum / steel siding**: less common; typically on older homes or commercial
- **Wood siding**: cedar lap or shake; higher maintenance; niche restoration market
- Siding damage from hail is frequently overlooked by adjusters — it's a major supplement opportunity

### Window Replacement
- **Double-pane standard**: $450–$900 per window depending on size, glass type, and frame material
- **Triple-pane / upgrade glass**: $700–$1,200+ per window; noise reduction and energy efficiency selling points
- **Full-frame vs. insert/insert**: insert (frame stays) is less disruptive but not always appropriate after structural damage
- Custom sizing to 1/16" tolerance is a quality differentiator; mass-market contractors quote rough openings and use insert methods
- Window damage from hail or wind is frequently co-mingled with roof and siding damage on the same claim

### Gutter Installation
- Seamless aluminum gutters: $8–$15 per linear foot installed; $1,200–$3,500 for a typical home
- Leaf guard / gutter covers: $15–$30 per linear foot; increasingly requested as a value-add
- Often damaged in wind events; gutter damage claims are frequently under-scoped by initial adjusters

### Exterior Painting
- Power washing, priming, and repainting of trim, shutters, and fascia boards damaged by hail or wind
- Typically $3,000–$12,000 for a full exterior repaint; often bundled into a siding claim if hail impacts are documented
- Color consultation is a value-add selling point

### Interior Water Damage Remediation
- **Water extraction + drying**: $2,000–$10,000 depending on sq footage and severity
- **Mold remediation**: $2,000–$15,000+ if mold is present; often missed in initial adjuster scope
- IICRC (Institute of Inspection, Cleaning and Restoration Certification) is the industry standard credential for water damage firms
- Restoration companies often partner with independent remediation firms rather than doing this in-house
- Moisture meters and thermal imaging cameras are essential diagnostic tools for this work

### Storm Damage Repair (General)
- Tarping, board-up, and temporary protective measures immediately after a storm
- Structural assessment to determine if framing, sheathing, or load-bearing elements are affected
- Scope of work often expands beyond initial adjuster estimate as hidden damage is discovered

### Hail Damage Claims
- Hail causes bruising, granule loss, and dents to shingles; circular impact patterns are the hallmark
- Hail damage to siding (dents in aluminum, punctures in vinyl, spattering on fiber cement) is often invisible from ground level
- Hail damage to windows (spontaneous breakage, stress cracks in annealed glass) is a separate line item
- Insurance carriers use software-driven estimating (Xactimate) to price hail repairs; supplement negotiation is routine

### Wind Damage Claims
- Wind uplifts shingles, tears underlayment, and can remove entire roof sections
- Debris impact damage (tree branches, flying objects) is typically classified as wind or "falling object" rather than a separate event
- Wind damage disputes are common — carriers often attribute damage to wear rather than the storm event; documentation is critical

### Complete Exterior Restoration
- Full scope: roof + siding + windows + gutters + trim — often triggered by major storm events (tornado, hurricane, severe hail)
- Projects can range from $40,000 to $150,000+ for full exterior replacements on larger homes
- These are the highest-value jobs; contractor credibility and claim management skill are decisive factors
- Scope documentation and Xactimate line-item precision are essential for full recovery

### Insurance Claim Negotiation / Advocacy
- Reading and interpreting insurance estimates (RCV vs. ACV calculations)
- Supplement submissions for items missed in the initial scope
- On-site adjuster meetings ("field adjusters") to walk the property together
- Depreciation holdback recovery (recoverable depreciation)
- Dispute and appraisal process support
- This is the primary differentiator between a restoration contractor and a retail roofer

## 3. Typical Customer Pain Points

### Claim Denied or Underpaid
- This is the single most painful and most common complaint in the insurance restoration space
- Homeowners feel the insurance company is adversarial; they want someone on their side
- Key pain: "I know there's more damage but I don't know how to prove it"
- Key pain: "The adjuster was here for 20 minutes and wrote an estimate that doesn't cover the actual cost"
- The supplement process and on-site adjuster meeting are the core value propositions

### Fear of Contractor Overcharging
- Homeowners have been burned by retail contractors and storm chasers who over-invoice insurance
- They worry that if the invoice exceeds the insurance payment, they'll be on the hook
- Answer: explain the Xactimate process, show itemized estimates, and position as working within insurance constraints
- "Your out-of-pocket cost is limited to your deductible" is the most powerful objection-killer

### Storm Chaser Scams
- After major storms, out-of-town contractors flood neighborhoods with door-to-door pitches
- Horror stories circulate: contractors take deposits and disappear, do shoddy work, over-invoice insurance (fraud)
- Homeowners are primed to be suspicious of any contractor who approaches them after a storm
- "We'll find you through your insurance company or a referral — never at your front door" is a critical differentiator

### Property Damage Documentation
- Most homeowners don't know how to document storm damage
- They take photos but miss the key evidence: hail indentations on vents, bruising on shingles, dented gutters
- Drone photography and professional documentation before the adjuster arrives is a high-value service
- "We document everything using the same standards insurance companies use" builds confidence

### Finding a Reliable Local Contractor
- Google search for "roofing contractor Manassas VA" returns hundreds of results, many of them national lead-gen farms
- Homeowners want someone who will answer the phone, show up, and still be there in 5 years if something goes wrong
- Local reviews, local references, and a local address are the three things that overcome this fear

### Negotiating with Insurance Adjusters
- Adjusters are trained professionals representing the insurance company — the homeowner feels outgunned
- The idea that "I can meet the adjuster on-site and advocate for the homeowner" is a powerful positioning
- "We've done this thousands of times — we know what to look for and how to present it" reduces homeowner anxiety

### Timeline Anxiety
- "How long until my home is fixed?" — insurance timelines are unpredictable
- Homeowners fear being under-covered mid-project if materials costs rise
- Clear process steps and typical timelines reduce this anxiety; "here's exactly what happens and when" is reassuring

### Coverage Questions
- "Is this actually covered by my policy?" is asked in some form by nearly every first-time caller
- Most standard HO-3 policies cover "sudden and accidental" damage — storms qualify
- Education on Actual Cash Value vs. Replacement Cost Value is almost always needed
- A free claim review CTA ("find out what's covered — free") captures these leads

## 4. Pricing Positioning Strategies

### How Insurance Restoration Pricing Works
- Insurance restoration is NOT the same as retail contracting; the insurance payment structure changes the conversation
- **Homeowner's responsibility**: Their deductible (typically $500–$2,500 for standard HO-3 policies)
- **Insurance payment**: Covers the difference between the agreed scope and the deductible, minus any depreciation holdback
- **RCV (Replacement Cost Value) policies**: Insurance pays full repair cost; homeowner pays only deductible; recoverable depreciation paid after completion
- **ACV (Actual Cash Value) policies**: Insurance pays current value (depreciated); homeowner absorbs the gap — this is a critical distinction to explain
- The contractor's price must match or support the Xactimate scope; inflated pricing raises red flags with carriers

### Writing Desk / Estimate vs. Actual Xactimate Pricing
- **Writing desk estimates**: informal estimates created without proper documentation; insurance companies don't accept these
- **Xactimate**: The industry-standard estimating software used by most major insurance carriers (EA Claims, ISO templates)
- Xactimate prices are published and reviewed annually; contractors who know the software can audit and supplement claims
- "We use Xactimate — the same software your insurance company uses" is both a trust signal and a technical differentiator
- Line-item precision matters: a missed line item = lost revenue for the contractor; missed items are common in initial adjuster estimates

### "We'll Cover Your Deductible" Competitors
- This is an illegal practice in many states (insurance fraud); in others it's a grey area
- Legitimate operators must not waive deductibles — it constitutes insurance fraud by inflating the claim
- Position against it: "We never waive deductibles — that's illegal and it puts your claim at risk"
- A competitor's "free deductible" offer should trigger: "That contractor is either overcharging your insurance (fraud) or cutting corners on materials"
- The honest answer: "Your deductible is your responsibility — we work within the system so your insurance pays their fair share"

### Supplement Claims / Negotiation Fees
- Supplementing is the industry standard for insurance restoration: submitting additional line items that were missed in the initial scope
- There is typically no additional fee for supplements in most contractor agreements — it's built into the pricing model
- Some companies charge a "supplement preparation fee" — this should be disclosed upfront
- The threat of a supplement is sometimes used by carriers to delay approval; experienced contractors know how to manage this

## 5. Trust Drivers

### Insurance Certifications
- **Xactimate**: Not a certification exactly — it's software proficiency. Contractors who are Xactimate-certified have undergone training and typically produce estimates that carriers accept more readily
- **HAAG Certified (HAAG Engineering)**: Covers both residential and commercial roof damage assessment; highly recognized in the industry; says the contractor knows how to identify and document damage
- **IICRC**: For water damage and mold remediation; 5-track certification program; widely recognized
- **GAF Master Elite**: Only ~3% of contractors qualify; requires proper licensing, insurance, and customer satisfaction track record; offers the Golden Pledge Warranty
- **CertainTeed SELECT Shingle Master**: Requires ongoing training and demonstrated installation quality
- Display these with brief explanations: "What this means for you: [one sentence in plain English]"

### State Licensing Requirements (Virginia)
- Virginia requires contractors to be registered if doing $1,000+ in work
- Class A contractor license (Virginia): no limit on job size; requires exam, experience, and insurance
- Class B: up to $120,000 per project
- Class C: specialty trades (roofing, electrical, plumbing)
- Maryland and DC have separate requirements; cross-state operators must be licensed in each jurisdiction
- "Licensed & Bonded in VA, MD & DC" is a meaningful trust signal for NoVA contractors

### BBB Ratings / Google Reviews
- BBB A+ rating is achievable and highly valued; respond to every review, positive and negative
- Google Reviews are more impactful than BBB for local service searches — target 4.7+ stars with 50+ reviews
- Display the Google rating as a badge on the homepage and in the footer
- Photo reviews (before/after with the homeowner visible) are especially powerful
- Yelp and HomeAdvisor / Angi are secondary; Google is primary

### Local vs. Storm Chaser Differentiation
- **Storm chaser** = out-of-town contractor that descends on a market after a major storm, does volume work, and leaves
- Warning signs: door-to-door solicitation, 800 numbers, no local address, appeared within months of the storm
- Local operator differentiators: local phone number (703 area code), physical address, years in the market, local references
- Messaging: "We're your neighbors. Our office is on [street]. We're here 20 years from now when you need us."

### Manufacturer Certifications
- **GAF Master Elite**: Offers Golden Pledge Warranty (up to 25 years on materials + labor); only ~3% of roofers qualify; consumer-recognized brand
- **CertainTeed SELECT Shingle Master**: Ongoing education requirement; signals quality installation
- **James Hardie Preferred**: Fiber cement siding installation; the James Hardie brand is consumer-recognized
- **Pella Elite**: Windows; less common for restoration contractors but signals premium capability
- Manufacturer certifications are trust signals for homeowners who know to ask; they're also required by many manufacturers to honor the warranty

### Proper Licensing and Bonding
- General liability: $1M minimum; $2M recommended; protects the homeowner's property during work
- Workers' compensation: Required in Virginia; protects homeowner from liability if a crew member is injured
- Commercial auto insurance: Covers company vehicles
- Bonding: Indicates financial stability; some jurisdictions require bonds for public projects
- Display proof: "Full Certificate of Insurance available upon request" or redacted certificate image on website

### Workman's Comp Importance
- In Virginia, if a contractor doesn't carry workers' comp and a worker is injured on your property, the homeowner can be sued
- This is a top objection from informed homeowners; having it stated prominently ("Full workers' comp on all crew members") removes a major trust barrier

## 6. Common Objections + Responses

### "My insurance already offered me X"
**Response**: "That's a common starting point. Most initial estimates don't include everything — hidden damage is almost never documented on the first pass. What I'd like to do is come out, do a thorough inspection, and compare it to what your insurance offered. If there's a gap, we'll submit a supplement. This is standard in insurance restoration — the initial estimate is rarely the final word. And there's no cost or obligation to you."

**Why it works**: Reframes the insurance offer as incomplete rather than final. Positions the contractor as a supplement expert.

### "I got 3 other quotes cheaper"
**Response**: "I understand — and I want you to feel confident in your decision. Can I ask: were those estimates based on an Xactimate scope or a desk estimate? And did they include the full scope — gutters, trim, siding if applicable? In insurance restoration, comparing bids without comparing line-item scopes is like comparing recipes without knowing the ingredients. I'd like to walk you through exactly what we'd be doing so you can compare apples to apples."

**Why it works**: Questions the comparability of other bids; introduces Xactimate as a quality differentiator; offers to educate rather than dismiss.

### "Storm chasers gave me a better price"
**Response**: "I'd be careful with that. Here's why: storm chasers who come into an area after a storm often use inferior materials, understaff crews, and leave before any issues surface. If something goes wrong in 2 years — a leak, a warranty claim — they're not there. We're at [local address], we're here year-round, and we stand behind every project. The 'better price' often comes with a 'worse product.' Would you like me to show you our warranty terms compared to what they're offering?"

**Why it works**: Leverages the storm chaser fear directly; contrasts local permanence with transient operators.

### "I'll wait until I have more damage"
**Response**: "That's your call — I respect that. But here's something to consider: most policies have a deadline for filing after a loss, and if the storm is aging out of your memory, documentation becomes harder. Also — if you wait, wear and tear starts to blend with storm damage, and your insurance company can start attributing future issues to age rather than the event. If you have any damage at all, a free inspection documents it now while it's fresh. If there's nothing there, you're out nothing."

**Why it works**: Introduces urgency without being aggressive; the free inspection offer reduces friction.

### "I don't trust contractors"
**Response**: "That's fair — and honestly, some of that distrust is earned by the behavior of others in this industry. Here's what I can tell you about us: we started in [year], we have [X] projects on record, we carry [insurance details], and we're [BBB rating / Google rating]. I'll give you references — homeowners in [city] whose project is done — and you can call them directly. We also provide a written workmanship guarantee. What would it take for you to feel comfortable moving forward?"

**Why it works**: Acknowledges the objection rather than dismissing it; provides concrete proof points; offers references (most powerful trust tool available).

### "Is this really covered?"
**Response**: "That depends on your specific policy, and I'm not an insurance expert — but most standard HO-3 policies cover sudden and accidental damage from storms, including wind and hail. What I can do is come out, document the damage thoroughly, and then help you understand what your policy covers based on what we find. If the damage is there, the coverage question becomes clearer. If it's not, you're out nothing. Either way, you'll have clarity."

**Why it works**: Doesn't over-promise on coverage; offers a practical path to finding out; uses free inspection to convert uncertainty into action.

## 9. FAQ Patterns (Insurance Restoration Specific)

### Will Insurance Cover This?
Most standard homeowners policies (HO-3) cover sudden and accidental damage from storms, including wind damage and hail damage. Flood damage (from rising water) requires separate flood insurance. Fire and lightning are typically covered under standard policies. The best way to know for certain is a free inspection — we document the damage and help you understand your coverage based on what's actually there.

### How Does the Claims Process Work?
After a storm, you file a claim with your insurance company (we can help with this). An adjuster is assigned and schedules an inspection. We recommend calling us BEFORE that inspection — we'll document all damage thoroughly so nothing is missed. We can also meet the adjuster on-site to advocate for a complete scope. Once the estimate is approved, we begin work. Insurance typically pays in two tranches: initial payment and recoverable depreciation after completion.

### What If My Claim Was Denied?
You have the right to appeal — and in many cases, denials are based on incomplete documentation, not actual absence of damage. We use Xactimate estimates backed by industry standards to document all damage, and we'll meet with adjusters directly to support your claim. If the claim is ultimately denied after the full appeal process, you owe us nothing for the inspection and estimate.

### How Long Does It Take?
Most residential insurance restoration projects are completed within 1–3 weeks of approval. The insurance claims process (from first call to work starting) typically spans 3–6 weeks depending on claim complexity and carrier responsiveness. We provide a clear timeline at the estimate stage and keep you updated throughout.

### What If I Already Have an Adjuster's Estimate?
That's actually the best time to call us. Initial adjuster estimates frequently miss hidden damage, underprice line items, or exclude related components (e.g., gutters, window frames, flashing). We compare our inspection against the adjuster's estimate and submit a supplement for any gaps. This is standard practice in insurance restoration and is how contractors ensure the scope reflects the actual damage.

### Do I Have to Pay My Deductible?
Yes — your deductible is your responsibility under your policy. This is standard. What we do is make sure your deductible is the ONLY thing you're responsible for. Some less-reputable contractors offer to "cover your deductible" — this is insurance fraud. We work within the system honestly and transparently, and we make sure insurance pays their full portion so you're not left covering unexpected costs.

### What's Xactimate?
Xactimate is the industry's leading property estimating software — it's the same software most major insurance companies use to price claims. Estimates are built line-by-line (each shingle, each square of material, each labor hour) using industry-standard rates. When we say we use Xactimate, it means our estimate is in the same language as your insurance company's — which makes the negotiation clearer and more transparent.

### Can I Choose My Own Contractor?
Absolutely. You have the legal right to choose your own contractor for repairs. Your insurance company may recommend preferred vendors, but the final decision is yours — and no contractor should make you feel otherwise. Modern Remodeling works for YOU, not the insurance company. That's the most important difference.

### How Do I Know If I Have Storm Damage?
Most storm damage — especially hail damage — isn't visible from the ground. Dents in shingles, bruising on underlayment, and hail impacts on siding are easy to miss without a close inspection. We use hands-on assessment and drone technology to identify all affected areas. If neighbors have damage from the same storm, there's a high probability you do too.

### Will Filing a Claim Raise My Rates?
In most cases, no. Storm damage is typically classified as an "act of God" and is considered a area-wide event. Insurance rates are more likely to be affected by area-wide loss history than by individual claims. Your individual claim history matters, but a single storm repair claim from a major weather event is generally treated differently than a property damage claim from a personal incident.

## 10. Insurance Restoration Industry Nuances

### HAAG / Xactimate Certifications
- **HAAG Engineering**: Trains and certifies inspectors in residential and commercial roof damage assessment. HAAG-certified inspectors are recognized by insurance carriers as having the technical expertise to identify and document damage properly. HAAG certification requires exam and continuing education.
- **Xactimate**: Software training is available through Xactware (a Verisk company). Many restoration contractors are trained in Xactimate but there's no formal certification that consumers see. The practical value: a contractor who knows Xactimate inside and out can write estimates that match or exceed carrier pricing expectations, submit supplements correctly, and avoid the rejection of legitimate line items.
- Together: HAAG documentation + Xactimate pricing = a complete and defensible claim package

### ACV vs. RCV Policy Differences
- **ACV (Actual Cash Value)**: Pays the current value of the damaged item — replacement cost minus depreciation. If a 20-year-old roof is destroyed, ACV pays what that 20-year-old roof is worth today, not what a new roof costs. Homeowners with ACV policies bear the gap between the depreciated value and the cost of replacement out of pocket.
- **RCV (Replacement Cost Value)**: Pays the full cost to replace the damaged item with a new one — without accounting for depreciation. The recoverable depreciation (the gap between ACV and RCV) is paid after the work is completed and verified.
- **How customers get confused**: Carriers sometimes pay ACV initially and leave the homeowner thinking the full claim is settled. Homeowners don't know to ask for RCV until the supplement is filed. Contractors who explain this clearly earn enormous trust.
- **Contractor's role**: Ensure the scope of work is priced at RCV rates; document depreciation properly; file for recoverable depreciation after completion; handle any ACV/RCV disputes with the carrier

### Supplement Claims Process
- A supplement is a request for additional payment on a claim that was under-scoped in the initial estimate
- Supplements are submitted by the contractor (not the homeowner) directly to the insurance carrier
- Common supplement triggers: missed line items (sheathing, flashing, trim), under-priced line items (labor rates, material grades), additional damage discovered mid-project
- The supplement process is not adversarial — it's part of the standard insurance restoration workflow
- Carriers have supplement review processes; experienced contractors know how to package supplement requests so they get reviewed promptly
- Multiple supplements on a single claim are normal and expected in complex restoration projects

### Depreciation Holdback
- With RCV policies, insurance carriers pay the ACV amount initially; the recoverable depreciation is held back until the work is completed
- After completion, the contractor invoices for the full RCV amount; the carrier releases the held-back depreciation
- Homeowners sometimes think the claim is fully paid when the initial check arrives; contractors must explain the two-payment structure upfront
- Recoverable depreciation can be substantial — on a $20,000 claim, the holdback could be $8,000–$12,000

### Homeowner Deductible Considerations
- Deductible is non-negotiable and cannot be waived by the contractor (this is law in most states, fraud in others)
- The contractor's price should reflect the full Xactimate scope; the homeowner pays their deductible to the contractor and the insurance pays the rest
- "We cover your deductible" is a red flag — either the contractor is committing insurance fraud (overcharging the carrier) or they're using inferior materials to make up the difference
- Some contractors offer "deductible financing" — this is legitimate and can be a helpful conversion tool; terms should be clearly disclosed

### Insurance Restoration vs. Retail Roofing
These are fundamentally different business models:

| Factor | Insurance Restoration | Retail Roofing |
|---|---|---|
| Customer's primary concern | "Will insurance cover it?" | "What's the price?" |
| Sales process | Education + claim advocacy | Price and product comparison |
| Pricing basis | Xactimate line items | Bid / estimate |
| Customer acquisition | Insurance carrier referrals, storm events, local SEO | Search, ads, cold outreach |
| Margin model | Supplements + full scope recovery | Competitive bidding |
| Sales cycle | Longer (insurance timeline) | Shorter (direct to project) |
| Repeat business | Lower (storm-driven, episodic) | Moderate (maintenance, referrals) |
| Trust bar | Very high (homeowner is vulnerable) | Moderate (price-driven) |

Understanding this distinction is essential: a retail roofer doing insurance work will miss supplements, mishandle adjuster meetings, and under-sell the scope. Insurance restoration specialists know the system.

### Storm Season Demand Patterns

| Season | Demand Level | Key Triggers | Contractor Opportunity |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Late Winter (Feb–Mar)** | Low-moderate | Ice storms, freeze-thaw cycles | Ice damage inspections; pre-season roof maintenance |
| **Spring (Apr–Jun)** | High | Severe thunderstorms, hail, tornadoes | Peak storm season; hail events drive volume |
| **Summer (Jul–Aug)** | Moderate | Tropical storms, heat damage (thermal cycling) | Repair work from spring storms; supplemental claims |
| **Early Fall (Sep–Oct)** | High | Hurricane remnants, nor'easters | Major storm events; fall is second peak season |
| **Late Fall (Nov–Dec)** | Low-moderate | Winter storm prep; early snow events | Pre-winter inspections; supplemental claim submissions |
| **Winter (Jan–Feb)** | Low | Ice storms, snow load damage | Emergency repair; burst pipe (water damage) work |

- Major storm events (hurricanes, tornados, widespread hail) create backlogs of 4–8 weeks for non-emergency restoration work
- The "storm chasing" window is typically 2–6 weeks after a major event — contractors who mobilize fast capture volume
- For established local operators, the advantage is trust, not speed — storm chasers take work that local contractors turn down (because it's substandard or fraudulent); local operators can pick the best jobs

### What "Scope of Work" Means in Insurance Context
- Scope of work (SOW) = the complete list of all damaged items and repair tasks required to restore the property to pre-loss condition
- The scope is documented in Xactimate with line items: each material, each unit of measurement, each labor allocation
- The scope is negotiated between the contractor and the insurance carrier; initial scope ≠ final scope
- A thorough scope includes: all visible damage + all hidden damage (documented through inspection) + all related and连带 damage (e.g., damaged flashing when replacing shingles)
- Scope disputes are common; the contractor's documentation and Xactimate proficiency are decisive
- "Pre-loss condition" is the legal standard — not "better than before" (upgrade) but "as good as before the loss"
